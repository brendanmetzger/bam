Sound Opinions

2013, 2020

soundopinions.org

Sound opinions is a nationally syndicated radio program and podcast.

Data, sans database

It is perhaps no big surprise that the producers of Sound Opinions adhere to NPR affiliate cliché of having a firm appreciation for egg-headedness. When approaching an organization as a collection capable humans, one should take the opportunity to design for that organization. In my case, I pursue designs that rely as heavily as they can on the human end of the bargain, though this can only be accomplished by placing firm expectations on that same end. And a wee note, whenever I say the word 'design' in the context of anything I do, I care little for the arrangment of items and layouts—I'm referring to the action one takes to perform a task and realize a goal.

The inherited content from the old site had been crafted; artists, albums, places, etc. had been noted meticulously. The site they were preparing to leave behind was 100% old-school html—a format I truly and deeply value. The riddle was thus: how do you keep the uniqueness of custom authored markup but enhance it with a management system that makes creating new content, adding metadata, and searching and sorting content easy (for syntax enthusiasts, of course). Given that the goal is to end with markup, why not just start with markup? Of course, that means putting all your trust in the human...

Outcome

I prototyped a single-text-box cms to show the possibility of using only syntax to create episodes—an idea that, quite naturally, was met with suspicion. That quick prototype turned out convincing enough, and interestingly, that work never changed all that much in the final production version.

A user simply creates an outline like they would in a word processing program. For complicated endeavors, like media-management, syntax was provided to work with every api imaginable: soundcloud, youtube, flickr and iTunes. Copy a linkt from YouTube and you have a player, or to Flickr and you have a gallery.

For a site to provide context and navigability an admin provides metadata,—usually in a series of menus, checkboxes and other inputs. In observing people fill out forms for a decade, I've never gotten the impression that it is an enjoyable activity. But metadata can be enjoyable if it saves you time, especially if you already did the hard parts of authoring some content. In a nutshell, the Sound Opinions CMS tracks metadata by asking that an admin to look over the copy and highlight significant elements (like an album or artist name). They can link the bits of highlighted text to one another by literally drawing a line between them. Some patterns have meaning—connecting an album and and artist triggers a query to find and embed album art. I daresay, it's almost fun.
